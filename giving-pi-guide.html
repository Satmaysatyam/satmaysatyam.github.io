How to not get a bad PI

Choosing the Right Lab
A Quick Note: While these guidelines apply to joining a lab for a Master's, PhD, or Postdoc project, they are especially critical when choosing a PI for a longer-term commitment, such as a PhD.

🔍Phase 1: Pre-Interview & Lab Evaluation

🔬Evaluating New Labs

Verify recent publications are based on data from current student projects, not just papers from the PI's previous work. Additionally, ensure the PI has genuine expertise in the specific project they are offering. Sometimes new PIs pitch completely novel projects outside their wheelhouse, which leads to poor supervision—a shortcoming they may try to overshadow by unfairly criticizing the student for a "lack of literature appreciation."

📊Analyze Lab Demographics

Take a close look at the demographic makeup of the lab. If you notice significant deviations or a lack of diversity, pay attention. Privately ask former students if there is a culture of favoritism, or if they have experienced or witnessed any discrimination based on region or gender within the lab.

💬Talk to the Lab Members

Privately contact current and former students. Their answers are often more revealing than any interview question.

🤝Phase 2: The Interview & Aligning Expectations

🗣️What to Ask During the Interview

Ask about expected hours, equipment support, data access, and what happens when field conditions delay work. Crucially, ask them directly what their expectations are from a student, how they handle manuscript writing and review, and how accessible they are for day-to-day queries. Clarify timelines, communication style, and what success looks like in the first 3, 6, and 12 months.

⏳Check Timelines and Milestones

Ask how long it usually takes for students to complete their PhD in that specific lab. Additionally, inquire about major milestones like the comprehensive exam (a standard requirement in Indian universities)—find out exactly how much time past students took to clear it. Consistent delays in these milestones can be a major roadblock for your career.

🧭Assessing Mentorship Styles

Match your working style to the PI: micro-managed, hands-off, or balanced. Not every style suits every student and every PI.

🚀Phase 3: Navigating Your Project

📅Insist on Regular Update Meetings

Once you begin, establish a strict schedule for project update meetings with your PI. Aim for at least one meeting per month, and insist that these happen in person whenever possible. Consistent check-ins keep your research moving and prevent you from being left stranded when roadblocks arise.

📝Document Everything (Create a Paper Trail)

Always confirm project decisions over email. If something goes wrong, a PI might deny giving prior authorization or permission. Protect yourself by sending brief "minutes of the meeting" after every discussion (including your monthly updates), asking the PI to reply if any points were missed or incorrect. This creates an invaluable, timestamped proof of communication.

✍️Discuss Authorship Upfront

Before starting any project, have a transparent conversation about authorship. Establish exactly who will be on the author list and what their respective positions will be. Be vigilant; some PIs have a bad habit of gifting authorship to people they know who provided no significant input or actual work on your project.

🕳️Watch Out for Communication Black Holes

If you find that many of your important emails are being consistently ignored and getting a simple reply is a hectic job that takes weeks—especially if it's a new PI with very few administrative duties—take it seriously. This is a major red flag. If you are dealing with this early on, I would strongly suggest finding a new PI. You will thank me later.

🚩Summary: Major Red Flags to Watch Out For

Red Flags: Vague project ownership or unclear funding; gifting authorship to non-contributors; favouritism or discrimination based on gender or region; a habit of ignoring important emails for weeks; no written expectations; dismissive answers to basic questions; extreme difficulty in scheduling one-on-one meetings; and inconsistent or frequently cancelled lab meetings.

⚖️New vs. Established PIs: Weighing Your Options

🌱Joining a New PI

The Traditional View (Pros): It is generally assumed that new PIs start with fresh funding, fewer administrative burdens, and a high level of passion. Because they are building their lab, they often have more time to dedicate to supervising their first few students.

The Reality Check (Cons): This is not a guarantee. Some new PIs take on projects outside their expertise or struggle with communication and funding. You must vet a new PI rigorously using the pre-interview and lab evaluation criteria above before committing.

🏛️Joining an Established PI

The Traditional View (Pros): Established labs offer stability, a proven track record, and a wealth of supervisory experience and technical expertise.

The Potential Drawback (Cons): Because their careers are already solidified, some senior PIs may have lower motivation for individual student projects or simply be too busy to offer hands-on guidance.

The Reality Check: This is absolutely not a universal rule. You will find highly established PIs managing massive labs (with multiple PhDs, postdocs, and heavy administrative roles) who remain incredibly available, deeply invested in every student's work, and offer excellent, personalized supervision. Judge the individual, not just the career stage.
